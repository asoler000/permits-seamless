Executive Summary

Seamless utilizes commercial permit activity as a highly accurate timing intelligence system, not as generic lead generation. The system operates by identifying commercial locations with recent HVAC and critical system work, predicting likely post-installation issues, and proactively engaging facility leaders. Crucially, the system prioritizes existing relationships first, ensuring that high-probability opportunities are strategically routed into meaningful maintenance conversations rather than cold spam.

Closed vs. Open Permits

  • Closed permits = PRIMARY maintenance trigger / act now
  • Open/issued permits = SECONDARY awareness trigger / prepare, warm, map relationships

Correct framing: "Closed permits = act now. Open permits = prepare."

4. Permit Filtering Logic

The 3-Tier System

  • Tier 1 Primary Triggers: HVAC / mechanical permits, HVAC replacements, RTU installs, major HVAC equipment swaps
  • Tier 2 Secondary Triggers: Substantial electrical, plumbing, signage (multi-location signage maintenance programs), multi-trade permits
  • Tier 3 Context Signals: Broader commercial renovation permits that may support awareness but not primary outreach

Signage as a Valid Trigger

Important Update: Signage permits ARE a valid Tier 2 trigger.

Signage permits signal an opportunity for recurring signage maintenance programs. Multi-location brands need ongoing signage maintenance, repair, compliance work, and brand consistency management. This is a high-value recurring revenue stream separate from facility maintenance.

Exclusions

The following must be excluded from the trigger logic:

  • Residential properties
  • Fencing
  • Purely cosmetic work (non-signage)
  • Lighting-only upgrades (unless part of signage)
  • Tiny one-off repairs
  • Irrelevant minor permits

5. Permit Stages

Closed (Primary Trigger)

Systems are installed. The maintenance window begins. Target outreach timing is 15-60 days post-close.

Issued / Active (Secondary)

Project is currently in progress. Use this data for awareness and relationship mapping only.

5.5 Three-Tier Permit Priority System

Strategic Filter Question:
"Does this permit indicate a system that will need ongoing maintenance?"

Phased Rollout Plan

  • Phase 1: HVAC only
  • Phase 2 (after 30 days): Add electrical + plumbing

Note: Multi-trade renovations become premium/high-value signals.

Example Scoring Logic (Prose)

A closed HVAC permit at a restaurant location 30 days post-close represents the highest priority signal (e.g., 10/10). A substantial electrical permit at a retail store ranks slightly lower but remains a valid secondary trigger. A plumbing permit at a restaurant is highly valuable, whereas the same plumbing permit at a retail store represents a lower priority. Renovation context signals only elevate scores if tied to Tier 1 or Tier 2 trade work.

8. Relationship Validation (CRITICAL)

Validation Check Hierarchy

Before any outreach occurs, run these checks sequentially:

  1. HubSpot — do we know them?
  2. RFMA / ConnexFM — existing contact or warm network?
  3. Previous work history?

IF YES: Route directly to rep for relationship-based follow-up.
IF NO: Proceed to outbound email sequence.

This is the edge. Relationship intelligence beats cold automation.

9. Scoring Model

The system evaluates markets based on a maximum total of 50 points.

5-Part Scoring Formula

  1. Permit Data Accessibility = 0-10
  2. Chain Restaurant / Retail Density = 0-10
  3. Construction Activity Volume = 0-10
  4. HVAC Criticality (climate) = 0-10
  5. Vendor Network Viability = 0-10
TOTAL = 0-50

What the Totals Mean

45-50 Excellent - start here
40-44 Very good - launch early
35-39 Good - expand later
30-34 Okay - maybe someday
Below 30 Weak - skip for now

Examples

  • Dallas-Fort Worth = 45/50 (9 + 9 + 10 + 9 + 8)
  • Seattle = 38/50 (10 + 7 + 9 + 5 + 7)

10. Priority Markets - Strategic City Selection

Dense population alone is not enough; signal quality matters more. Markets are selected based on the 5-part scoring formula defined above.

Tier 1: Start Here (Top Priority)

  1. Dallas-Fort Worth, TX: Best pilot, top construction activity, strong permit systems, chain density, hot climate.
  2. Austin, TX: Strong data access, rapid growth, hot climate, tech/service economy.
  3. Miami, FL: Major construction volume, hot-humid climate, dense retail and restaurant market.
  4. Orlando, FL: Exceptional QSR/chain density, tourism/hospitality intensity, HVAC mission-critical.
  5. Phoenix, AZ: Extreme heat, strong permit activity, HVAC failure = real business pain.

Tier 2: Expansion Markets

  1. Houston, TX: Massive industrial and commercial base.
  2. Atlanta, GA: High chain density and major southeastern hub.
  3. Nashville, TN: Rapid commercial expansion and hospitality growth.
  4. Chicago, IL: Dense urban retail and restaurant concentration.
  5. Seattle, WA: Excellent data accessibility despite milder climate.

Tier 3: Strategic Expansion Markets

  1. Tampa, FL: Strong climate need, secondary to Miami/Orlando.
  2. Charlotte, NC: Growing financial and retail hub.
  3. Los Angeles, CA: High volume but complex regulatory/data environment.
  4. San Francisco Bay Area, CA: Good data, but challenging operational economics.
  5. New York City, NY: Massive volume, but highly complex service network requirements.

Rollout Roadmap

  • Months 1-2: Pilot DFW
  • Month 3: Validate with Austin + one Florida or Phoenix market
  • Months 4-6: Complete Tier 1
  • Months 7-12: Expand into Tier 2 based on results
Avoid List: Markets with bad data, no chain density, no service network, or overcomplicated early launches.

12. Outreach Strategy

The "Do Not" List

  • Do not mention permits directly
  • Do not say congratulations
  • Do not sound generic or automated
  • Do not skip relationship checks

Email Structure: Context → Operational insight → Problem awareness → Simple question.

Note: Automate the system, not the voice.

Template 1: HVAC / Restaurant

"Hey [Name] —

Looks like the [City] location just went through some HVAC updates.

With kitchen environments, those systems tend to get stressed pretty quickly after install—heat, grease, runtime.

Are you guys handling that internally or with a service partner?"

Template 2: HVAC / Retail

"Hey [Name] —

Looks like the [City] location recently wrapped HVAC work.

After installs like that, systems usually start needing attention pretty quickly—especially in high-use environments.

How are you handling HVAC maintenance there today?"

Template 3: Electrical / Restaurant

"Hey [Name] —

Saw the [City] location had some electrical work done recently—looks like panel upgrades or new equipment hookups.

With kitchen operations, electrical systems take a beating—especially with the constant load cycling from ovens, walk-ins, and ventilation.

How are you guys staying ahead of that maintenance-wise?"

Template 4: Electrical / Retail

"Hey [Name] —

Looks like the [City] store went through some electrical infrastructure work recently.

With retail operations, electrical reliability isn't optional—lighting, HVAC, POS systems all need to stay up.

Are you working with anyone on ongoing electrical maintenance, or handling it as issues come up?"

Template 5: Plumbing / Restaurant

"Hey [Name] —

Noticed the [City] location had plumbing work done—looks like it might've been grease trap or water heater related.

Those systems are critical in restaurant ops, but they're also the ones that tend to need the most attention post-install.

What does your maintenance cadence look like for plumbing right now?"

Template 6: Multi-Trade Renovation

"Hey [Name] —

Looks like the [City] location went through a pretty significant renovation recently—HVAC, electrical, and plumbing all got attention.

Projects like that usually mean the location's positioned for higher volume, which also means those systems are going to work harder than they did before.

How are you thinking about maintenance for that site moving forward?"

Template 7: Signage Maintenance Program

"Hey [Name] —

Saw the [City] location had signage work done recently.

With multi-location brands, signage is one of those things that needs consistent attention—lighting failures, weather damage, code compliance, brand consistency across locations.

Are you guys handling signage maintenance reactively, or do you have a program in place across your portfolio?"

17. AI Implementation Prompt

Provide the following prompt to your developer or AI builder to execute the architecture:

Build a structured system for a facility maintenance company (Seamless FM) that uses commercial permit data to generate maintenance opportunities.

=== CORE SYSTEM REQUIREMENTS ===

1. PERMIT FILTERING - 3-TIER PRIORITY SYSTEM:
   - Tier 1 (Primary Triggers): HVAC/Mechanical, RTU replacements, major HVAC equipment
   - Tier 2 (Secondary Triggers): Substantial electrical (panel upgrades, equipment installations), 
     Plumbing (grease traps, water heaters, major line work - especially restaurants), 
     Signage (multi-location signage maintenance programs), Multi-trade permits
   - Tier 3 (Context Only): Building renovations that include Tier 1/2 components
   - EXCLUDE: Residential, fencing, minor cosmetic (non-signage), lighting-only (unless signage)

2. PERMIT STAGE INTELLIGENCE:
   - CLOSED permits = PRIMARY trigger (systems installed, maintenance window begins)
   - Outreach timing: 15-60 days post-closure
   - OPEN/ISSUED permits = SECONDARY (awareness, relationship mapping, preparation)

3. BUSINESS TYPE FOCUS:
   - Priority: Restaurant/QSR (kitchen-heavy operations), Retail (climate control critical)
   - Multi-location brands strongly preferred
   - Chain restaurants/retail = highest value

4. RELATIONSHIP VALIDATION (CRITICAL - CHECK BEFORE ANY OUTREACH):
   Step 1: Check HubSpot CRM (existing clients, history, notes, prior work)
   Step 2: Check RFMA/ConnexFM contact lists (industry relationships)
   Step 3: Check previous work history
   IF RELATIONSHIP EXISTS → Route to rep for relationship-based follow-up
   IF NO RELATIONSHIP → Proceed to enrichment + outbound email

5. DATA ENRICHMENT:
   For each record, append:
   - Brand name and multi-location status
   - Business type (restaurant vs retail)
   - Contact data: Facility Manager, Director of Facilities, Regional Ops (director+ level)
   - Climate/region type (see section 6 below)
   - Environmental factors (see section 7 below)
   - Seasonal considerations (see section 8 below)

6. CLIMATE INTELLIGENCE (Critical for HVAC scoring and messaging):
   - HOT/HUMID (Miami, Orlando, Houston): HVAC = survival, year-round critical, 
     high stress from constant runtime + humidity
   - EXTREME HEAT (Phoenix, Las Vegas): 120°F+ summers, HVAC failure = immediate closure, 
     cooling loads extreme May-September
   - HOT DRY (Dallas, Austin): 100°F+ summers, dust/pollen stress, 
     high cooling demands 6+ months/year
   - MODERATE (Seattle, SF, Portland): HVAC less critical, heating/cooling balanced
   - Tag each location with climate zone for contextual outreach

7. ENVIRONMENTAL STRESS FACTORS (Add to enrichment):
   - Airport proximity: High particulate load, frequent filter changes needed
   - Freeway/highway proximity: Diesel particulates, accelerated filter degradation
   - Coastal locations: Salt air corrosion, accelerated equipment wear
   - Industrial areas: Heavy particulate environment
   - High pollen regions: Seasonal filter stress (spring/fall)
   - Urban density: Higher particulate loads than suburban

8. SEASONAL INTELLIGENCE (Add time-based context to outreach):
   - Spring (Mar-May): Pollen season stress, transition to cooling mode, 
     'systems are about to work harder'
   - Summer (Jun-Aug): Peak cooling loads, 'HVAC is running 24/7 now'
   - Fall (Sep-Nov): Second pollen season, transition stress, 
     'before winter hits'
   - Winter (Dec-Feb): Heating mode, restaurant kitchens battle indoor air quality
   - Include seasonal context in message generation based on current date

9. CITY-SPECIFIC MARKET INTELLIGENCE:
   Use 50-point scoring system (5 criteria × 10 points each):
   - Permit Data Accessibility (1-10): Can we get clean, timely data?
   - Chain Restaurant/Retail Density (1-10): Concentration of target buyers
   - Construction Activity Volume (1-10): Number of relevant permits
   - HVAC Criticality (1-10): Climate impact on system importance
   - Vendor Network Viability (1-10): Can Seamless service this market?
   
   TIER 1 PRIORITY CITIES (45-50 points - Start Here):
   - Dallas-Fort Worth, TX (45/50): Best pilot, #1 construction volume, hot climate
   - Phoenix, AZ (44/50): Extreme heat = HVAC critical, top 3 construction
   - Austin, TX (44/50): Best data access, hot climate, fast growth
   - Miami, FL (43/50): Top 3 construction, hot/humid, dense chains
   - Orlando, FL (43/50): #1 QSR density nationally, tourism = no downtime tolerance
   
   TIER 2 EXPANSION CITIES (38-40 points):
   - Atlanta, Houston, Nashville, Chicago, Seattle
   
   Launch Strategy:
   - Months 1-2: Pilot DFW only
   - Month 3: Add Austin + Miami/Phoenix
   - Months 4-6: Complete Tier 1
   - Months 7-12: Expand to Tier 2

10. OPPORTUNITY SCORING MODEL:
    Score each opportunity based on:
    - Permit Type (HVAC = 10, Electrical = 7, Plumbing = 6, Signage = 6, Multi-trade = 10)
    - Business Type (Restaurant = 10, Retail = 8)
    - Permit Status (Closed = 10, Issued = 5)
    - Days since closure (15-60 days = 10, 61-90 = 7, 91-120 = 5)
    - Climate criticality (Hot/Humid = 10, Extreme Heat = 10, Hot Dry = 9, Moderate = 6)
    - Multi-location brand (Yes = 10, Unknown = 5)
    - Environmental stress factors (Airport/Freeway nearby = +2 bonus)
    
    Highest priority: HVAC + Restaurant + Closed 15-60 days + Hot climate + Multi-location = 50+

11. OUTREACH MESSAGE GENERATION:
    Generate context-aware messages using:
    - Permit type (HVAC, Electrical, Plumbing, Signage, Multi-trade)
    - Business type (Restaurant vs Retail)
    - Climate context ("With [city] heat..." or "In kitchen environments...")
    - Environmental factors ("Given your proximity to [airport/freeway]..." if applicable)
    - Seasonal context ("With summer coming..." or "After the spring pollen season...")
    - System-specific stress factors:
      * HVAC Restaurant: heat, grease, runtime, kitchen load
      * HVAC Retail: high-use, customer experience, can't afford downtime
      * Electrical Restaurant: load cycling, ovens, walk-ins, ventilation
      * Electrical Retail: lighting, HVAC, POS systems, reliability critical
      * Plumbing Restaurant: grease traps, water heaters, critical systems
      * Signage: lighting failures, weather damage, brand consistency, code compliance
      * Multi-trade: positioned for higher volume, systems working harder
    
    MESSAGE RULES:
    - DO NOT mention permits directly
    - DO NOT say "congratulations" or sound generic
    - DO reference recent activity indirectly ("looks like", "noticed", "saw")
    - DO mention operational reality and post-install stress
    - DO show expertise through specific system knowledge
    - DO ask simple, consultative questions
    - DO include climate/seasonal context when relevant
    - DO mention environmental factors if applicable
    
    7 TEMPLATE FRAMEWORKS:
    1. Restaurant HVAC: Kitchen heat + grease + runtime stress
    2. Retail HVAC: High-use environments + customer experience
    3. Restaurant Electrical: Load cycling + kitchen equipment stress
    4. Retail Electrical: Lighting + HVAC + POS reliability
    5. Restaurant Plumbing: Grease trap + water heater criticality
    6. Multi-Trade: Significant renovation = higher volume positioning
    7. Signage: Lighting + weather + compliance + brand consistency

    - Brand name

=== STRATEGIC PRINCIPLES ===

- This is NOT lead generation. This is timing intelligence.
- Relationships first. Always validate before cold outreach.
- Climate matters. HVAC in Phoenix ≠ HVAC in Seattle.
- Context = expertise. Generic emails lose. Specific operational knowledge wins.
- Quality > volume. 10 perfect opportunities > 100 mediocre ones.
- Start narrow (DFW only), prove it works, then scale deliberately.
